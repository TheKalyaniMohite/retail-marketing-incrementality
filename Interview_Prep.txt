 INTERVIEW PREP — Retail Marketing Incrementality Project
 "Why" Behind Every Technical Decision
================================================================================

This file is your cheat-sheet for marketing analyst / data analyst interviews.
Each section explains a design choice so you can speak confidently about it.


If a recruiter asks about this project, use this STAR method script:

Situation: "I wanted to measure the true ROI of a marketing campaign without relying on biased tracking pixels."

Task: "I designed a geo-experiment comparing 5 Test cities against 5 Control 'twins' to isolate the effect of ad spend."

Action: "I used Python and the CausalImpact library to build a Bayesian counterfactual model, proving exactly what would have happened without the ads."

Result: "The analysis revealed a 159% ROI and identified that 12% of conversions were organic. I recommended a budget shift that would optimize $250k in quarterly spend."

STEP 1 — DATA GENERATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Why did you use synthetic data instead of real data?
A: Real campaign data is proprietary and rarely available publicly.  Synthetic
   data lets me control every variable — I know the EXACT lift I injected, so I
   can validate that my model recovers it.  In an interview I'd say: "I designed
   the ground truth so I could benchmark the model's accuracy."

Q: Why Twin Cities?
A: Twin cities are the backbone of a quasi-experimental design.  We can't run a
   true A/B test across entire metro markets, so we pick city pairs that are
   structurally similar (population, climate, retail mix).  The control city
   acts as the counter-factual — "what would have happened WITHOUT the ad?"

   Pairs and rationale:
     • SF ↔ Seattle       — West-coast tech hubs, similar demographics.
     • Austin ↔ Denver    — Fast-growing Sun Belt / Mountain metros.
     • Miami ↔ Phoenix    — Warm-weather cities with seasonal tourism.
     • Chicago ↔ Boston   — Large, established Midwest/Northeast metros.
     • Dallas ↔ Atlanta   — Major Southern business hubs.

Q: Why these specific data features (seasonality, weekly effects, holidays)?
A: Real retail sales are NEVER a flat line.  If my synthetic data looked too
   clean, any model would trivially detect the lift.  By layering in:
     1. Annual seasonality  — captures the natural holiday ramp-up.
     2. Day-of-week effects — weekends outsell weekdays by ~13%.
     3. Holiday spikes      — Black Friday, Christmas, etc.
     4. Gaussian noise      — random daily variation.
   … the data mirrors real POS/transaction data.  This forces the model to
   separate the CAMPAIGN signal from the SEASONAL signal — which is the entire
   challenge of incrementality measurement.

Q: Why does the campaign lift ramp up over 7 days?
A: Ad campaigns don't flip a switch.  There's ad-serving latency, customer
   awareness buildup, and purchase-decision lag.  A 7-day ramp is realistic and
   tests whether the model handles gradual treatment onset.

Q: Why different lift percentages per city (15-22%)?
A: Markets respond differently.  A 22% lift in Austin but only 15% in Miami
   reflects real-world heterogeneity.  This lets us discuss "heterogeneous
   treatment effects" in interviews — a concept increasingly asked about in
   marketing analytics roles.

Q: Why set a random seed (np.random.seed(42))?
A: Reproducibility.  Anyone cloning the repo gets identical data.  This is a
   professional engineering practice — interviewers notice it.

STEP 2 -- EDA & PRE-INTERVENTION VALIDATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Why do you need EDA before running the causal model?
A: "Trust but verify."  EDA serves two purposes:
     1. Sanity-check the data -- are there missing days, outliers, or broken
        patterns that would corrupt the model?
     2. VALIDATE THE PARALLEL TRENDS ASSUMPTION -- this is the foundational
        assumption of Diff-in-Diff and CausalImpact.  If the twins don't
        co-move before December, the whole study is invalid.  I prove they
        do with correlation and t-tests.

Q: What is the Parallel Trends Assumption?
A: It means: "In the absence of the treatment, the test and control groups
   would have continued to move together."  Visually, the twin-pair
   time-series lines should overlap from Jan-Nov.  Statistically, a high
   Pearson correlation (>0.99 in our case) and a non-significant paired
   t-test (p > 0.05) confirm this.

Q: Why Pearson Correlation specifically?
A: Pearson r captures linear co-movement, which is exactly what we need.
   Our results show r > 0.99 for all five twin pairs -- "Excellent" match.
   This is what makes the control city a credible counterfactual.

Q: Why a Paired T-Test on weekly averages?
A: Two reasons:
     1. PAIRED -- because each week in the test city has a natural match
        in the control city (same calendar week).  An unpaired test would
        throw away this structural alignment.
     2. WEEKLY AVERAGES -- daily data is noisy.  Aggregating to weeks gives
        ~48 paired observations, which is enough for the t-test's normality
        assumption to hold (Central Limit Theorem).
   All five pairs gave p > 0.05, confirming NO significant pre-intervention
   difference -- exactly what we need to proceed.

Q: What charts did you produce and why?
A: Eight charts, each serving a specific analytical purpose:
     1. 5x Twin-pair time-series (7-day rolling avg) -- visual parallel trends.
     2. Combined test vs control overview -- shows the aggregate divergence
        in December and makes the "story" immediately obvious to a stakeholder.
     3. Pre-intervention scatter plots -- if twins are well-matched, points
        cluster along the 45-degree line.  The r-values printed on each
        subplot give instant credibility.
     4. December bar chart comparison -- isolates the campaign month and
        prints the raw lift percentage per pair.

Q: Why use a 7-day rolling average on the time-series?
A: Daily sales are noisy; the rolling average smooths out the day-to-day
   fluctuations so the TREND and SEASONAL components (the signals we care
   about) become visually dominant.  It's the difference between a chart
   that tells a story and one that looks like static.

STEP 3 -- CAUSAL INFERENCE (DiD + CausalImpact)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Why TWO causal methods (DiD AND CausalImpact)?  Isn't one enough?
A: Using two independent methods is a ROBUSTNESS CHECK.  If both methods
   agree (and ours do -- within ~3%), it dramatically strengthens the claim
   that the effect is real, not an artifact of one model's assumptions.
   In an interview: "I triangulated with two methods to increase confidence."

Q: What is Difference-in-Differences (DiD)?
A: DiD compares the CHANGE in the test group to the CHANGE in the control:
     DiD = (Test_post - Test_pre) - (Control_post - Control_pre)
   If the campaign had NO effect, both groups would change equally and DiD = 0.
   Any non-zero DiD is the estimated causal effect.
   We formalize it as an OLS regression:
     Sales = b0 + b1*Treatment + b2*Post + b3*(Treatment x Post)
   b3 IS the DiD estimator.  Our b3 = $1,713/day with p = 1.95e-23.

Q: What is CausalImpact (Bayesian Structural Time-Series)?
A: Google's approach.  It builds a model of the test city using the control
   city as a predictor during the pre-period.  Then it PREDICTS what the test
   city WOULD HAVE DONE in December without the campaign (the counterfactual).
   The gap between actual and predicted is the causal effect.
   Key advantage: it gives you a FULL TIME-SERIES of the effect, not just
   one number, plus Bayesian credible intervals for uncertainty.

Q: What is a "counterfactual" and why does it matter?
A: The counterfactual is "what would have happened if we hadn't run the ad."
   We can never observe it directly -- it's the road not taken.  The entire
   point of the twin-city design is to ESTIMATE this counterfactual using
   the control city.  CausalImpact makes this explicit as a prediction line.

Q: Why did you use HC1 (robust) standard errors in the OLS regression?
A: Daily sales data is heteroscedastic -- variance changes across cities and
   seasons.  Ordinary standard errors would be too narrow, inflating
   significance.  HC1 (Huber-White) standard errors are robust to this,
   giving honest p-values and confidence intervals.

Q: What are bootstrapped confidence intervals and why use them?
A: Bootstrap = resample the data with replacement, refit the model 1,000
   times, and take the 2.5th/97.5th percentiles as bounds.  It's
   distribution-free (doesn't assume normality) and accounts for model
   uncertainty.  Our 95% CI for total incremental revenue is
   [$249k, $270k] -- entirely above zero, confirming the effect is real.

Q: How do you calculate ROI?
A: ROI = (Incremental Revenue - Campaign Cost) / Campaign Cost x 100
        = ($259,324 - $100,000) / $100,000 = 159%
   Every $1 spent returned $2.59.  With the 95% CI: ROI is [149%, 170%].

Q: What's the 3-panel CausalImpact plot showing?
A: Panel 1 (Original): Actual test city sales vs the counterfactual
   prediction.  The gap in December IS the causal effect.
   Panel 2 (Pointwise): The daily dollar difference (actual - predicted).
   Shows the ramp-up and variation in effect size.
   Panel 3 (Cumulative): Running total of the effect.  The final value
   is the total incremental revenue attributable to the campaign.

Q: Did both methods agree?
A: Yes.  DiD estimated ~$1,713/day pooled; CausalImpact estimated
   $1,473-$1,946/day per city.  The agreement validates both approaches
   and strengthens the overall conclusion.

STEP 4 -- RESULTS VISUALIZATION & INTERACTIVE DASHBOARD
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Why build an interactive dashboard instead of just showing charts?
A: Three reasons:
     1. STAKEHOLDER COMMUNICATION -- executives don't read regression tables.
        An interactive dashboard lets them explore the data themselves, which
        builds trust in the findings.
     2. PORTFOLIO DIFFERENTIATION -- most candidates show static matplotlib
        charts.  An interactive, dark-mode dashboard with animated KPIs
        immediately stands out.
     3. SELF-SERVICE ANALYSIS -- the Twin-Pair Explorer lets the viewer
        drill into any city pair, not just the aggregate.

Q: Why Chart.js and not Tableau/Power BI?
A: For a portfolio project, a self-contained HTML file is ideal:
     - No licenses, no logins, no software installs needed.
     - Recruiters can open it in any browser in 1 click.
     - It demonstrates front-end engineering skills (HTML/CSS/JS) alongside
       data science skills -- a rare and valuable combination.
   In a corporate setting, I'd absolutely use Tableau or Looker for
   team-wide dashboards wired to live data sources.

Q: Why a dark-mode design?
A: Modern analytics dashboards (e.g., Grafana, Vercel Analytics, Linear)
   default to dark mode because:
     - Charts have better contrast against dark backgrounds.
     - It feels premium and data-focused.
     - It reduces eye strain for analysts reviewing data all day.

Q: What are the animated KPI cards doing?
A: They count up from zero to the final value when the page loads.
   This is a micro-animation technique that:
     - Draws the eye to the most important numbers first.
     - Creates a "reveal" moment that makes the data feel dynamic.
     - Is a standard UX pattern in dashboards (Stripe, Shopify, etc.).

Q: How is the dashboard generated?
A: A Python script (build_dashboard.py) reads the CSV results and injects
   the data directly into the HTML as JavaScript variables.  This means:
     - The dashboard is 100% self-contained (no API calls, no backend).
     - It can be hosted on GitHub Pages for free.
     - Data is "baked in" so it loads instantly.

Q: What story does the dashboard tell?
A: It follows the "inverted pyramid" storytelling structure:
     1. KPI cards -- the headline answer (ROI, revenue, lift) in 2 seconds.
     2. Time-series chart -- the "why" (visual divergence in December).
     3. Twin-Pair Explorer -- the "how" (interactive drill-down per city).
     4. Lift bars + ROI waterfall -- the "so what" (business impact).
     5. Detailed table -- the "prove it" (exact numbers with CIs).

STEP 5 -- EXECUTIVE SUMMARY & ROI CALCULATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What's the difference between ROI and ROAS?
A: Both measure return but from different perspectives:
     ROI  = (Revenue - Cost) / Cost x 100  = ($259k - $100k) / $100k = 159%
     ROAS = Revenue / Cost                 = $259k / $100k = 2.59x
   ROI tells you "how much PROFIT per dollar invested" (159 cents per dollar).
   ROAS tells you "how much REVENUE per dollar spent" (2.59 dollars per dollar).
   Marketing teams typically report ROAS; finance teams prefer ROI.
   Know both -- interviewers may use either term.

Q: Why include confidence intervals in the executive summary?
A: Confidence intervals communicate UNCERTAINTY, which executives need for
   decision-making.  Saying "we generated $259k" is point estimate bravado.
   Saying "we generated $249k-$270k with 95% confidence" is honest science.
   The CI never drops below $249k, which still means a 149% ROI in the
   worst case -- the campaign is profitable even at the lower bound.

Q: How do you frame the results for a non-technical audience?
A: Use the "inverted pyramid":
     1. Lead with the answer: "The campaign generated $259k, a 159% ROI."
     2. Support with one visual: the divergence chart.
     3. Address the skeptic: "We controlled for seasonality using twin cities."
     4. End with a recommendation: "Scale the budget for next quarter."
   Never start with methodology.  Executives want the answer FIRST.

Q: Why include recommendations in a measurement report?
A: A report without recommendations is just homework.  Actionable
   recommendations show you can THINK like a business partner, not just
   a data technician.  The four we included:
     1. Scale the campaign -- the ROI justifies it.
     2. Optimize underperformers -- Miami needs creative testing.
     3. Extend the framework -- use twin-city for other channels.
     4. Invest in always-on measurement -- move from periodic to continuous.

Q: Why generate both markdown and HTML report formats?
A: Two audiences, two formats:
     - Markdown (.md) renders beautifully on GitHub -- recruiters browse repos.
     - HTML (.html) is printable, shareable, and looks like a professional
       consulting deliverable -- interviewers may ask to "walk me through it."

Q: What limitations did you disclose and why?
A: Always disclose limitations proactively:
     1. Synthetic data -- shows intellectual honesty.
     2. Single campaign period -- acknowledges temporal scope.
     3. Geographic confounders -- shows you understand threats to validity.
     4. No channel decomposition -- identifies future work.
   Interviewers respect candor.  Hiding limitations is worse than having them.

================================================================================
 PROJECT-WIDE Q&A -- FOR THE "TELL ME ABOUT A PROJECT" INTERVIEW QUESTION
================================================================================

Q: Walk me through this project in 60 seconds.
A: "I built an end-to-end incrementality measurement system to answer one
   question: did our $100k ad campaign actually move the needle, or was the
   sales increase just holiday seasonality?

   I designed a twin-city quasi-experiment -- matching 5 test cities that
   got the ad with 5 control cities that didn't.  I validated the twins
   moved in lockstep for 11 months (Pearson r > 0.99), then measured the
   December divergence using two causal inference methods: Diff-in-Diff
   and CausalImpact.

   Both methods agreed: the campaign generated $259k in incremental revenue --
   a 159% ROI -- statistically significant at p < 0.001.  I built an
   interactive dashboard and an executive report to present the findings."

Q: What would you do differently with real data?
A: Several things:
     1. Use covariate matching (propensity scores) to select twin cities
        instead of manual pairing.
     2. Add external covariates (weather, competitor spend, macro-economic
        indicators) to the CausalImpact model.
     3. Run a power analysis BEFORE the campaign to determine sample size.
     4. Measure long-term effects (customer lifetime value, brand lift).
     5. Decompose by channel (search, display, social) to optimize mix.

Q: What's the one thing you're most proud of in this project?
A: The dual-method validation.  Running DiD AND CausalImpact and showing
   they converge is what separates "I googled it" from "I understand
   causal inference."  The triangle of evidence -- pre-period validation,
   two independent methods, and bootstrapped CIs -- gives stakeholders
   real confidence in the result.
